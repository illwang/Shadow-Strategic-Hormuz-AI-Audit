## Executive Summary (Buy-Side Lens)

Markets have priced HBM shortage and geopolitical risk as "known knowns." Observable signals suggest three layers remain under-modeled: (1) the L6 financial scissors gap from long-term compute lock-in contracts, (2) multilateral material weaponization expanding from bilateral Japan–Korea tension, and (3) water-resource red lines in Western China datacenter projects. Samsung's wage resolution (May 27, 73.7% approval, 95.5% turnout) has been priced as supply normalization, but allocation reportedly extends through 2027 with no backend-capacity increase observed post-vote. Anthropic's projected Q2 operating profit (US$559mn on US$10.9bn revenue) and the SpaceX S-1 disclosed US$45bn compute contract (through 2029) provide demand-side validation, but non-GAAP caveats and ramp-up discount magnitudes remain unobserved. The core tension axis is **contracted compute demand versus physically deliverable infrastructure capacity**.

**Sector tilt:** Supply-constrained nodes with direct qualification moats (HBM vendors, CoWoS advanced packaging) appear favoured over indirect-exposure names where OEM buffer layers may dampen revenue transmission. China domestic alternatives (Ascend ecosystem, CXMT HBM development, advanced packaging) may act as geopolitical bifurcation hedges. Cloud capex beneficiaries carry sustainability-risk premium if Q1 data reflects pull-forward rather than organic acceleration.

**Key catalysts to monitor:** Japan METI Korea material controls (Jun–Jul), Anthropic Q2 GAAP reconciliation (Aug), 2027 HBM allocation window closure (Jul–Aug), Western China datacenter EIA batch results (Q3).

## 1. What the Market Believes

Three consensus narratives are currently mispriced in AI infrastructure:

**Narrative 1 — "Samsung strike resolution = HBM supply normalization"**
The market treats the May 27 wage agreement (73.7% approval, 95.5% turnout) as a green light for supply-chain easing. This conflates labour peace with capacity availability. Industry checks confirm 2026 HBM output from all three major vendors is largely allocated, with order books extending into 2027. The resolution may have accelerated customer urgency to secure 2027 allocation before the pricing window closes.

**Narrative 2 — "Q1 capex extrapolation = sustainable AI demand run-rate"**
The four North American hyperscalers guided combined 2026 AI capex to approximately US$710bn+ (Q1 up 70.3% YoY). The market appears to be annualizing a quarter that may reflect IPO-window timing, pre-election project acceleration, and customer-concentration spikes. Anthropic's projected Q2 operating profit (US$559mn on US$10.9bn revenue) is directionally positive but contains non-GAAP adjustments and SpaceX ramp-up discounts that may not repeat in H2.

**Narrative 3 — "Nvidia ecosystem exposure = direct revenue for upstream suppliers"**
Market commentary frequently conflates OEM supply, indirect exposure, and ecosystem participation into a single "Nvidia revenue" figure. Company disclosures do not confirm direct attribution, and the fifth-largest customer represents only 2.1% of revenue for certain upstream names, suggesting significant OEM buffer layers that dampen sensitivity transmission.

## 5. Structural Drivers

- **Supply side:** Physical HBM capacity constrained by CoWoS bottleneck and memory wafer allocation; 2026 output largely committed. Greenfield not expected before 2027–2028.
- **Demand side:** Hyperscaler capex guided up; **Anthropic-SpaceX US$45bn compute contract (through 2029) confirmed in S-1 as demand-locking mechanism.**
- **Pricing mechanism:** Allocation-based pricing pressure elevated in constrained channels; vendor-side take-or-pay terms reportedly strengthening.
- **Constraints — Layer 1 (Financial):** Long-term compute lock-in contracts (Anthropic-SpaceX US$45bn) imply sustained demand, but whether this creates synchronized refinancing pressure across the sector in 2027–2028 is directional, not confirmed.
- **Constraints — Layer 2 (Material):** Japan's proposed 3-category export controls to South Korea would expand material weaponization from bilateral to multilateral. South Korea faces simultaneous Japanese material controls and U.S. technology controls.
- **Constraints — Layer 3 (Physical):** Western China datacenter water quotas approaching red lines under 74% liquid-cooling penetration. Water has shifted from a cost item to a permit item. Russian helium export controls through 2027 + Hormuz throughput at ~15% of baseline = geopolitically driven supply disruption, not a cyclical shortage.

- Samsung labour resolution removes immediate disruption, but allocation tightening continues.

- Memory names priced for scarcity persistence; indirect-exposure names may carry sensitivity overestimation.
- Gulf war-risk premiums have not been priced into sovereign datacenter or subsea cable financing models. A 200–300 basis point cost spike is plausible if Hormuz tension escalates.

## Risk Factors

1. **Efficiency revolution:** A commercially validated open-source model that cuts HBM demand by >40% sustained over two quarters would invalidate the supply-scarcity thesis.
2. **Capital flight:** If two consecutive quarters show cloud-provider AI capex contraction or operating-cash-flow coverage deterioration, the demand-validation chain breaks.
3. **Geopolitical escalation:** A Taiwan-strait incident or a full Japan–Korea material-export halt would trigger a supply shock beyond current pricing models.
4. **Regulatory reversal:** A US–China AI safety agreement or H200 licensing relaxation would compress the geopolitical-hedge premium on domestic Chinese alternatives.
5. **Water constraint activation:** If Western China datacenter water red lines trigger 12–18 month physical delays, cloud capacity expansion timelines may shift materially.
6. **Contract renegotiation risk:** The Anthropic-SpaceX US$45bn contract is confirmed via S-1, but any subsequent amendment, renegotiation, or early termination would materially alter the demand-locking narrative. This is an observable risk via SEC filings.
7. **Data limitations:** Certain price, transit, and water data points lack fully independent open-source verification paths and are flagged as directional rather than confirmed.
